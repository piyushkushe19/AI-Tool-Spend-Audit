# Go-To-Market — SpendLens

---

## Exact target user

**Primary:** Engineering Manager or CTO at a Series A startup, 15–40 people, $2M–$10M ARR. They approved AI tool subscriptions ad hoc as engineers requested them over 6–18 months and have never done a consolidation review. They see the monthly bill, feel vague unease, but don't have time to research alternatives.

**Secondary:** Founder-CEO at a seed-stage company (5–15 people) personally reviewing the monthly credit card statement and seeing $400+ in AI tools they don't fully understand.

**NOT:** Enterprise procurement teams (they have tooling), individual developers (they don't control budget).

---

## What they Google before they'd want this

They don't search for "AI spend audit tool" — that category doesn't exist in their heads yet. They search when facing a pricing decision:

- "cursor vs github copilot for teams cost comparison"
- "claude team plan worth it"
- "chatgpt enterprise pricing 2025"
- "reduce ai tool costs startup"
- "is cursor business plan worth it for small team"

These are high-intent queries from people already thinking about their AI spend. A blog post that ranks for "cursor vs copilot cost" and embeds the SpendLens widget is the ideal acquisition path.

## First 100 users in 30 days, $0 budget

**Week 1 (Day 1-3): Show HN**
Post a Show HN: "Show HN: Free AI tool spend auditor — find out if you're overpaying for Cursor/Claude/ChatGPT."

The HN copy: "I found myself approving AI tool requests for months without ever looking at the total bill. Built this to audit it in 60 seconds. Takes inputs for 8 tools, gives finance-grade reasoning. Curious what others find."

A front-page Show HN is realistic for a genuinely useful free tool in a developer-adjacent category. Front-page drives 500–3,000 unique visitors in 24 hours. At 40% form completion and 30% email capture: 60–360 email captures in Day 1.

**Week 1 (Day 3-5): r/ExperiencedDevs and r/startups**
Post: "I audited our AI tool spend and found $480/month in unnecessary subscriptions — here's the pattern I see a lot."

Write a genuine post about what I found in my own audit (honest data, not marketing). Embed the tool as a way to check your own stack. This performs better than "I built this tool" posts because it leads with the insight, not the product.

**Week 2: Twitter thread**
"We've analysed AI tool spend at 50 startups. Here's what we found:"
- 5 specific data points from real audit submissions (anonymised)
- "The most common waste: paying for Cursor Business with 1-2 users ($40/seat vs $20/seat)"
- "The most expensive mistake: running Anthropic API at $800/mo when Claude Team seats = $300/mo"
- Thread ends with tool link

Target: engineers and EMs who follow Lenny Rachitsky, Gergely Orosz, and similar accounts. Ask 2-3 friendly followers with 5k+ relevant audiences to repost.

**Week 3-4: Content seeding**
Write one blog post: "The AI Tool Spending Audit I Wish I'd Done 6 Months Ago." Publish on Hashnode or dev.to (indexed by Google). Rank for "cursor vs copilot cost", "claude team vs pro", "ai tool audit."

**Expected Week 4 state:** 150–300 email captures, 10–20 Credex consultation bookings (from high-savings audits).

## The unfair distribution channel

Credex has direct relationships with AI enterprise vendors — they source excess credits from these companies. Those same companies' enterprise sales teams have customers who just bought subscriptions they're now questioning. 

Credex can walk into Cursor's or Anthropic's enterprise customer community and say "we built a free tool to help you right-size your subscription" — and be trusted, because they're a known partner. No competitor can claim that relationship.

Specific execution: ask Cursor/Anthropic/OpenAI enterprise teams to share SpendLens with customers during onboarding. The tool helps customers use the product correctly (right-sized plan) and generates warm inbound for Credex's credit marketplace.
